## Post 1 - [Posted five days ago]
The average tech seller earns about $50 an hour.

That's the math behind a $100,000 year.

But here's what most reps never realize:

Everything you do in a day falls into one of four buckets:

- $10 an hour: updating the CRM.

- $100 an hour: following up on deals.

- $1,000 an hour: running real discovery with a qualified buyer.

- $10,000 an hour: the work almost nobody makes time for.

That top bucket is the one that builds careers and, for a few people, real wealth.

Most reps spend almost no time there. 

Maybe one hour a month. 

Sometimes one a quarter.

So what counts as $10,000-an-hour work?

Selling to the economic buyer. 

The one person who can actually release the budget.

When you get good at it, the math changes fast.

According to Gong, access to the economic buyer can lift your win rate by 130% on deals over $50,000.

Run the numbers. 

A 20% win rate becomes a 46% win rate.

That alone moves you into a different income bracket.

I'm not promising you $100,000 a month. 

Nobody serious would.

But the skill of selling to power is how you earn your first, or your next, $100,000 commission check.

The reps who figure this out stop trading hours for dollars and start trading skill for outcomes.

## Post 2 - [Posted a week ago]
If I could only ask 5 discovery questions that sell (and couldn’t do research), here they are:

(the order here matters) 👇



1. Tell me about your biggest challenges when it comes to X?

Easy enough.

Just enough to kickstart the conversation in the right direction.

And get to the 'heart of the matter' fast.

But not enough by itself. 

Customers will (almost) always give surface level answers to the first question.



2. What's going on in the business that's driving [what they shared] to be a priority.

Ask this, and your customers will CHUCKLE half the time.

Why?

Because you are striking a CHORD when you ask that.

You're getting to the 'need behind the need.'

That's where big money lives.



3. What metric is suffering most as a result of that?

Here's where most sellers struggle:

Quantifying pain.

You walk into a different world when you go from expressed pain to quantified pain.

Your customer's urgency ramps up.

And spending money to solve the problem begins to look REAL good.



4. What are the ripple effects this challenge is having on the business?

You’re looking for negative consequences.

The kind that heighten urgency.

The kind that increase willingness to spend.

The kind that increase the perceived size of the problem.



5. Who else is impacted by these challenges, and how?

This has the same impact as the last question.

With one addition.

It helps you multi thread.

It helps you get a sense of the influencers.

And it helps you get in touch with them later.

Give those 5 questions (in order) a try.

## Post 3 - [Posted a year ago]
My first year in SaaS sales, I earned $40,000. My last year in SaaS sales, I was lucky to earn $1.63M. 20 sales cheat codes I wish I knew when I started:

1. Buyers spend more $$ to relieve pain than to achieve gain. Stop obsessing about benefits. Start obsessing about problems.

2. The biggest opportunity your competition is probably not leveraging is deep multi-threading. Swarm your deals.

3. The demo your product team teaches you never works. They mean well, but they are too interested in making customers aware of every feature. Only show the features that solve problems. Leave off the rest.

4. One of the best sales hacks is responding fast. Responding fast to inbound lead. Responding fast to emails. Buyers mirror your speed.

5. Personalization will help you book more outbound meetings. But there are diminishing returns. Adding way too much personalization creates a lot of work and doesn't add to conversion.

6. Offering an 'end of month' discount only ever works if your buyer has total control over pulling the trigger. If they have to get approval from others, go through legal, procurement, etc. it's not going to move the needle.

8. Spend more time getting new influencers up to speed on past conversations you've had with their colleagues. 

9. A great question will always have more power than a great statement.

10. People don't buy because of ROI. ROI doesn't create demand. It justifies demand. 

11. Perfect sales technique will not close the deal if you're talking to the wrong person. I still remember the best discovery call I've ever run. I didn't close the deal. Wrong person.

12. Social selling isn't better than cold email. Cold email isn't better than cold calling. These channels work together. Leverage them as 'land, sea, and air.' Stop thinking binary.

13. If you can describe your buyer's problem better than they can describe it, their lightbulbs will turn on. It's beautiful. Buyers favor the seller that can best articulate their problems.

14. Get to know your market like the back of your hand. If you know your buyers so well that you can peer into their soul, you'll win.

15. Buyers never reveal the real problem after the 1st or 2nd question. Uncovering the 'need behind the need' is the closest thing to a magic bullet.

16. Execs want to know you did your homework. If you show up to a sales call with a C-SUITE exec and ask a generic question, you'll lose them.

17. Never negotiate price until it's the last issue. If agreeing on price doesn't close the deal, it's too early to negotiate.

18. Every time you demo a feature, preface it with the problem. You'll double your demo success with this change.

19. Stop blaming buyers for falling short. If they said they were going to sign last month, and they didn't, your fault. Not theirs.

20. The best demos are more about the customer than the product.

## Post 4 - [Posted ten months ago]
Two of the worst questions you can ask on a sales demo:

1. “Does that make sense?”
2. “What questions do you have?”

Those don’t sell. They just fill the silence.

✅ 10 demo questions that move deals forward:

1. “How are you doing [X workflow] today?”
(Ask right before you demo the feature → creates contrast between today vs your product.)


2. “How does that compare to how you’re doing it now?”
(Ask right after the demo → reinforces contrast, locks in value.)


3. “To what extent do you envision this being useful in your situation?”
(Gets them imagining themselves using your product.)


4. “How do you see your team using that?”
(Solid for decision makers who buy but don’t use.)


5. “To what degree is this resonating so far?”
(A mid-point pulse check. Much better than “any questions?”)


6. “To what extent do you see that solving [problem they shared earlier]?”
(Closes the loop between pain → solution.)


7. “What benefits do you see showing up in your world from this?”
(Gets them to voice the benefits out loud.)


8. “Where do you want to double-click with a few questions?”
(Same intent as “any questions?” but phrased as a pattern interrupt.)


9. “It seems like that resonated—what challenge in your business makes that hit home?” (When you strike a chord, this uncovers gold.)


10. “It seems like this isn’t resonating—where am I missing the mark?”
(When you’re off track, this pulls you back on track.)

The difference?

The first two questions put the spotlight on you.

The 10 above put the spotlight on them.

That’s where selling happens.
