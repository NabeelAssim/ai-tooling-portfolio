## Post 1 - [Posted six months ago]
There are plenty of products that do what your product does.

So why do people choose yours?

You don’t really know.
And neither do I.

The only people who do are the customers who already bought.

That’s why Jobs To Be Done interviews matter.

Talk to five people who purchased in the last 30 days, while the decision is still fresh and the story hasn’t been rewritten by time. You’re not looking for praise. You’re looking for the moment things shifted.

Ask them when they decided to buy.
Ask what they were using before they switched.
Ask when they first started looking for a solution.
Ask what changed that made staying put feel harder than changing.
Ask how they searched and evaluated options.
Ask what made them nervous about buying and what nearly stopped them.

Listen for hesitation.
Listen for trade-offs.
Listen for the language they use to describe the “before” and the moment it stopped working.

This matters because customers don’t buy products.

They hire them to escape something.

When you use the words you think sound good, outreach feels generic.

When you use the words they used to explain why they switched, it feels familiar.
And familiar is what earns replies.

Good messaging isn’t written.
It’s borrowed.

## Post 2 - [Posted six months ago]
Funny how numbers work in sales.

Here’s a number from a cold email I got:

“We’ve helped SaaS companies like X and Y reduce churn by 30% in six months through personalized retention strategies.”

Why is it always 30%?

Churn down 30%.
Revenue up 30%.
Productivity up 30%.
Happiness… probably up 30% too.

Always 30.
Never 27.
Never “it depended.”
Never “this part was messy.”

But real life doesn’t move in clean percentages.

Some customers stayed.
Some left anyway.
One change helped one segment and hurt another.
Some months were flat.

And 30% of what, exactly?

Logo churn?
Revenue churn?
Gross?
Net?

From what baseline?
Across how many customers?
In what situation?

When every number in a pitch looks polished, people assume the rough edges were sanded off.

Without context, it reads like a marketing number, not a believable one.

The brain registers it, nods, and moves on.

If the goal is credibility, numbers need an anchor.

For example:

“ACME and BETA cut net new monthly logo churn from ~3% to ~2% over six months by changing how renewal conversations were handled.”

Now I can picture it.
I can compare it to my own reality.
I can decide if it’s relevant.

Show the wobble.
Name the tradeoffs.
Talk about what didn’t work.

When everything goes up 30%, nothing feels true.

## Post 3 - [Posted ten months ago]
How to succeed in sales.

Simple.
But not easy.

Have 5 conversations a day with the people that are similar to your best customers. 

Not five emails.
Not five videos.
Conversations.

Conversations without an agenda.
Conversations that illuminate a potential problem without stearing people to a desired answer.

Like this:

“Not sure about you, but many vets tell me they lose 6–8% of revenue each year when pet owners buy online or at superstores. How are you dealing with that at your clinic?”

That’s not pitching.
That’s humble curiosity. 

The more conversations you have with your ideal prospects, the more money you make.

The fewer you have, the less money you make. 

The phone is not a cactus.
