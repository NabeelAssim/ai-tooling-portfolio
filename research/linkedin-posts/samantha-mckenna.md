## Post 1 - [Posted two weeks ago]
I met my husband's friends for the first time in 2016 at a party. A BOLD choice for a third date, but I digress...

We left and Ryan said, "They LOVED you!" 

As peachy as I occasionally am, I said, "I know why - it's because I spent the night getting to know them and asking questions about their lives."

It's a super power of mine and while occasionally alarming to some, my love of asking questions has served me in my personal life, but so much so in sales.

NPR did a great bit on the likeability of people who ask questions, but...

...how they are exponentially more likeable if they ask active listening questions. 

And, Forrester found that the number one thing B2B buyers want from their sellers is: 

Active listeners. Someone who asks a question, truly listens, and asks another based on the answer they heard (read: not one that is self-serving or qualifying).

So, if we know that asking active listening questions makes us more likeable, and that people buy from people they like, more often than not, it's a home run for all our sellers.

But the question is: where does it stack up on our training priorities? And have you considered the generational changes in how a 23 yr old communicates vs. a 53 yr old? It may come naturally to one and feel invasive to another. 

Seller, BDR, accountant, wealth manager, CSM...we can level up every person that engages with our buyers with just one tweak, and by teaching the art of great conversation. 

## Post 2 - [Posted two weeks ago]
Why does a post with 50 likes get 10,000 impressions but one with 300 gets 5,000, and what's a critical practice we run for our LI Exec Branding clients with the former?

The Math:

Take your impressions and divide them by your likes.

The output = your Dark Social Score.

Baseline benchmark = 40
Our standard aim = 50
Dark Social optimal traction = 70 (and any nominal increase over that)

10,000 impressions / 50 likes = 200 DSS 🔥 
5,000 impressions / 300 likes = 16 DSS 😢 

So, why this disparity? 

It's in the type of post you share.

The former tends to be pieces of content that shared something tactical, inspiring, actionable.
The latter tends to be celebratory.

The former gets lifted off of LI and shared in texts, Slack, etc. People show up (impression), read, look at your profile, and leave, all without engaging (hence the disparity).
The latter is your network celebrating you which is why your engagement is high and impressions are low. 

*******************************

Why Dark Social Matters:

You show up here for a reason: brand awareness. 

Most clients come to us to solve a few challenges:

- Their company or CXOs are relatively unknown
- Everyone knows their organization exists, but they're a large, faceless brand
- They're working to be the thought leaders of their categories - the ones turned to each time by journalists and conference organizers.

They show up to teach and for reach, and when that DSS sky rockets, it tells you that this is what your market is interested in, which is a data point to capitalize on. 

********************************

The CRITICAL step to capitalizing on your posts and DSS:

Operationalizing the GTM motions of your executives' brands.

Here's one of a dozen plays we run: systemize the review of your profile views each week, and connect with bespoke messaging to the 2nd and 3rd degree connections that are in your target ICP.

Not only will you systematically grow your network but you'll remove the gate that stood between them seeing your content the first time, you'll up the chances of them seeing the content again, and you'll create a drip campaign of content that they'll one day engage with, and will be seen by their network, full of people who look just like them.

## Post 3 - [Posted two weeks ago]
Self-deprecating humor is something we know, but what about the self-deprecating postscript that builds client relationships and might even help you get promoted faster?

Here's what I mean. When I make a mistake, you'll usually find me adding one extra layer that says, "I know better."

Sam: Can we meet at 1pm or 2pmET?
Buyer: Sure, what day though?
Sam: Ah, the date would be helpful, wouldn't it... :) Does Tuesday work?*

Boss: Can you shoot me the deck for tomorrow?
Sam: You bet, attached!
Boss: It has someone else's logo on it...?
Sam: Ah, thanks for the catch! Let me sweep the slides and make sure it's buttoned up - that would have been impossibly embarrassing.*

The normal answers I see are:

"Would Tuesday work?" without acknowledgement of the missed detail. It's small, but communicates volumes when not acknowledged.

and

"Fixed!" without acknowledgement that sending a deck with typos is sloppy or without proactively communicating the extra attention to detail.

When we skip that postscript, we communicate the wrong thing about ourselves, which is that we didn't know better, don't respect the person's time, or don't see the problem, none of which is something we want our leader or buyers to think.

Our buyers' trust will erode quickly.
Our leaders will feel the need to address basic professionalism in our 1:1

Instead, add the extra line, build humor in when you can, and triple check the work in the future. 

We all make 100 mistakes a day - it's what we do and how we proactively communicate afterwards that makes us different.
