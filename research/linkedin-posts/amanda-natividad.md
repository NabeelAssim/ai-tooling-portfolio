## Post 1 - [Posted a year ago]
Someone asked me why I post my marketing insights and POV so freely. Why I don't do more to capture leads. Honestly... I didn't have a great answer for it.

I finally just said, "Because... I think more people should just care about doing marketing thoughtfully, and I care more about making that impact than getting the form fill."

Do I want more followers? Sure. More newsletter subscribers, more podcast listeners, sure sure. More SparkToro customers? Yes, please. 😂 And more book buyers? YES.

But really, I just believe in this:

- Getting and sustaining attention is super hard, and it's only getting harder. 

- Big Tech scrapes all your content anyway, AI is only accelerating this, and it sucks for us but in the meantime we would be wise to optimize for impressions and ongoing engagement.

- It's becoming more and more important to know where your audience is actually hanging out online, what they care about, what they search for, and what topics they're interested in... so you can do marketing that actually resonates with them. In a way that is sustainable for your business and for your sanity. 

Ultimately, I care more about getting people to believe and practice these things than I do about squeezing a conversion out of every idea I share, and tracing it back to perfect attribution.

So yes, please buy my book and buy a SparkToro account. But also... it's ok if you don't. Just do your best to do thoughtful marketing, make smart bets, be good to your customers, unclench your jaw, and go drink some water.

## Post 2 - [Posted a month ago]
"B2B buyers are on LinkedIn" is true. It's also unsurprising. 

I ran an audience research report for mid-market RevOps leaders at B2B SaaS companies, and as you might've guessed, 80% of this audience uses LinkedIn. 

So should LinkedIn be in the plan? Of course.

But the more useful question is:

"Where does this audience find ideas they trust enough to repeat?"

RevOps leaders also over-index on Slack — 201% compared with the US average. But it isn't reasonable to like... go build a Slack marketing strategy.

More likely, they're using Slack because it's their company chat tool. This signals they likely work in fast-moving, cross-functional environments where internal conversations shape decisions. Unfortunately, you can't request an invite to their company Slack and start doing your marketing there (though if you can finagle that, I'd love to hear all about it).

What you can do is think about creating things worth bringing into those internal Slack conversations. The asset you create should not just be “clickable."

It should be forwardable.

Something a VP of Revenue can drop into Slack with:

"This is exactly what I’ve been trying to explain."

-or-

"Can we talk about this in our team meeting?"

That could be a:

• benchmark report
• calculator or diagnostic
• spicy-but-useful point of view
• teardown of a common RevOps mistake
• comparison guide that makes an internal debate easier

LinkedIn belongs in the plan. But it's not the whole strategy.

In this new weekly series, The Audience Research Brief, we pick an audience, run a SparkToro report, and show how we'd market to them.

Think of it as marketing strategy in a box.

## Post 3 - [Posted eight months ago]
Stop treating content like a vending machine. Start treating it like a service.

I call this method Content as a Service (CaaS) where every asset has:

- A client: the internal team it serves
- A job: the business outcome it drives
- A metric: how we’ll know it worked

This was how I ran my B2B content team at Fitbit. That simple mindset shift changed everything. Sales started coming to us first instead of last. Output went down — but impact went way up.

We weren’t asking, “What should we publish this week?”

We were asking, “What asset would actually shorten the sales cycle?”

That’s the difference between content for content’s sake and content as a service.

👇 I share the full framework (plus the CaaS map and intake brief template) in my latest SparkToro post. There’s also an audio complement to this advice — I recently discussed CaaS on Corey Wilks’ Creator Alchemy podcast.

## Post 4 - [Posted three months ago]
Zero Click Marketing does NOT mean zero sales.

People still need to buy things. They still need software, shoes, services. That demand doesn't disappear just because Google is answering queries in AI mode or because people are staying on Reddit instead of clicking through to your site.

What changes is how they decide who to buy from.

In Episode 3 of the Zero Click Marketing podcast, Rand Fishkin and I dig into:

- How to measure zero click efforts when you can't prove attribution
- Why execs accept fuzzy measurement for billboards but demand perfect attribution for content
- Rand's AI visibility research (spoiler: AI rank tracking is BS, but visibility percentage is real)
