## Post 1 - [Posted two years ago]
Traditional influencer marketing can be difficult to scale for B2B SaaS, because you might start running into things like: 

1. people only wanting to post about products they've used (it's easier to send people your new protein drink flavor than it is to onboard them to a $30k ACV software for free) 

2. sponsored posts not performing well because their audience expects a specific style of content from them (instead of a clearly-sponsored post) 

3. them running out of things to say after a few posts cause they're simply pushing your software (which is why people want to promote products they use, but again, see problem 1 above) 

So, I wouldn't rely on the traditional approach to sponsored posts (i.e. simply paying them to promote something) as my main way of working with B2B influencers.

Instead, you should have each external creator (whether an SME or a content creator) create their own episodic video series on a topic:

(a) they are interested in
(b) their audience expects from them 
(c) that is relevant to your business goals

And that way: 

--> they aren't creating content around your product, they are creating content around a topic they enjoy
--> their audience doesn't skip past a clearly sponsored post, but continues to engage with it as if it was their regular content
--> it plays its part in helping your brand build more mental recall since it associates your company with a certain topic, outcome, etc. 

Everybody wins. 

This is also a better way to approach sponsored posts if an influencer isn't creating an episodic video show for you. 

For example, when we started working with Dave Gerhardt at HockeyStack, instead of sending him some upcoming event or webinar to promote, I asked Emir Atli if we could send him our 'Stalking' commercial to post instead. 

Dave posted it, it got 800+ likes, 130+ comments, and 50+ reposts.

We got a lot of new (relevant) followers to our company page + site traffic, Dave had a sponsored post perform relatively well (almost as well as his normal posts), and his audience got to consume more awesome marketing content (what he already creates/what they expect). 

Everybody won.

So, the next post we sent him was our product rap, then an episode of one of our shows, and so on and so forth. 

So I think the best way to do B2B influencer marketing is to figure out what topic(s) or POVs overlap with:

(a) an external creator's interests
(b) their audiences expectations
(c) your narrative or business goals

And create/promote that. 

## Post 2 - [Posted a year ago]
one thing i still dont understand about B2B SaaS is why we havent completely leaned into gamification in our products 

i always think of Asana and how when you mark a task complete, a unicorn flies across the screen 

it's such a small thing, but i think the underlying purpose is to match what the user might be feeling in such moments by the product acknowledging the user's progress and accomplishments 

I think there is so much power in the product reciprocating and acknowledging the triumph and success the user might be experiencing in certain moments 

and that there are so many ways we can lean into this

- onboarding that provides in-product rewards 
- Playstation-style trophies you receive when hitting pre-determined milestones
- in-product notifications and animations when certain things are done, accessed, etc. 
- in-product avatars users can customize (like Reddit) 

also, you have to consider with alot of SaaS products making the move to credit-based pricing, gamification is inevitable since credit-based pricing provides SaaS products with the core aspect they were missing to make gamification truly work: in-game currency 

you could reward your product's currency when milestones are completed, trophies are unlocked, parts of onboarding are completed, and so much more

im thinking of making 'gamification consultation' one of my 4 services when I launch Side Quest and am interested in chatting with product and engineering teams and helping them figure out how they can go about gamifying their product 

i haven't done it before but i think this is a major part of future-SaaS and I want to start figuring it out 

## Post 3 - [Posted a year ago]
i don't agree with most things B2B SaaS companies do when it comes to influencer marketing 

but I also don't agree with how most of my peers approach B2B influencer marketing as individuals 

in particular, I'm referring to the "i don't promote products I don't use" mindset 

i think for a certain type of content creator, ones that make tactical videos of workflows they've created in the product, this makes a lot of sense 

but most creators aren't saying that from this perspective 

they're coming from the perspective of "being authentic" 

and I get it 

We onboard every influencer we sign to Clay (the company) into Clay (the product), we help them build Clay tables, etc.

ALMOST every influencer for Clay uses Clay 

why almost? 

because there's a certain type of creator (like me, like Aaron Morrissey, etc.), where, you're not hiring us to learn your product and create detailed breakdowns of feature sets

You're hiring us to get a specific message out there to more and more of your TAM in a way that they will not only consume but remember down the line

companies sign me to do 2 posts per month or 1 skit per month 

I'm not driving pipeline with 2 posts per month (i.e. to drive pipeline through content you need velocity, 2 posts/month is not velocity) 

what i can do though instead is make a conceptual video that associates your product with a specific outcome in a memorable way, resulting in significantly more people hearing and sharing your message 

it's almost like hiring an actor vs. hiring an expert 

you're not hiring me to create tactical videos of workflows I built (that's your SME influencers) -- they can create awesome videos about how your new feature works and all the outcomes you can achieve with it

you're hiring me to essentially create a really good commercial that let's your TAM know this feature exists in a way they will enjoy, share, and remember 

you need both 

embrace different types of creators and use them at their strengths 

a lack of this results in every influencer (whether a thought leader, SME, or content creator) thinking they need to become a product expert and all that 

I'm not going to forcibly learn your product when I'm not going to really use your product (I'm a simple dude with a very simple tech stack) 

instead, to determine whether i want to work with a company, I look at their ethics, business practices, how they treat employees, etc., and decide through these factors whether i want to partner with someone 

not whether I use the product or not

because the challenge for me doesn't lie in creating a tactical video breaking down feature sets

the challenge for me lies in seeing whether or not I can take your product and come up with conceptual content that will associate it with certain outcomes in memorable and creative ways 

so whether you're a brand or an influencer, I hope you found these thoughts helpful
