## Post 1 - [Posted nine months ago]
I’ve invested millions in startups.

B2B, SaaS, bootstrapped, VC-backed.

I’ve seen what chaotic growth looks like.

And I’ve seen what sustainable excellence feels like.

Here’s one thing I’ve noticed again and again:

The best CEOs aren’t the busiest.

They’re the clearest.

They’re not obsessed with managing time.

They’re obsessed with protecting attention.

The average founder jumps from:
• Slack messages
• Tactical 1:1s
• Product drama
• Team therapy
• Last-minute approvals
• Internal decks that no customer will ever see

They’re exhausted - but they’re not moving the business forward.

The best founders?

They operate differently.

What they all do:

1) They make time for non-urgent but essential work
– Hiring ahead of the curve
– Designing org structures that can scale
– Setting the tone and rhythm for the culture
– Thinking 6–12 months out

These aren’t “urgent” — but they compound.

Fast-growing companies that ignore them break later.

2) They aggressively delegate.
Not just tasks. Decisions. Ownership.
If someone can do it 80% as well, it’s theirs.
Micromanaging is just slow-motion sabotage.
And the best CEOs know that.

3) They remove themselves from fake productivity.
– Status meetings with no clear outcome
– Updates that could’ve been async
– Anything that smells like “looking busy”
Time is not just a resource. It’s a signal.
What you prioritize tells your team what matters.

4) And they stay close to the real leverage.
– Vision
– Capital
– Reputation
– Strategic hires
– Major crises
The stuff only the CEO can do, they own it fully.
They don’t try to do everything.
They make sure the right things get done — and only once.

The difference between good and great?

Not hustle.

Not IQ.

Not even luck.

It’s what you choose to ignore.

If you want to be a better operator, don’t just ask:

“What should I spend time on?”

Ask:

“What would the best CEOs I know never waste a second doing?”

Then act accordingly.

## Post 2 - [Posted seven months ago]
I get 50+ pitches a week. I delete 49.

Not because they're bad businesses. 

But because the founders already told me they'll fail.

Here's exactly what makes me hit delete:

"We have no competition" 
→ You haven't done research → Or you're in a market nobody wants → Either way, I'm out

"Our TAM is $50 billion" 
→ Cool → But you’ll capture 0 if you keep thinking top-down, not bottom-up → Show me the first 100 customers, not the last million

"We need funding to figure out our business model" 
→ No. You need customers 

"We're pre-revenue but..." 
→ There is no "but" → Charge someone. Anyone. Today. → $10 proves more than any pitch deck

"Our team has 3 MBAs from top schools" 
→ Great. Now show me what you've built → Credentials are lag indicators → I care about velocity, not pedigree

The emails I actually read?

"We're doing $20K MRR. Growing 30% monthly. Here's exactly how…”

Short. Specific. 

Most investors will tell you they invest in people.

Sure.

But I also invest in evidence.

And 98% of pitches are just sophisticated ways of saying "trust me."

I don't.

Show me you can build a business with constraints. 

Then I'll help you remove them.


## Post 3 - [Posted a year ago]
9 advice I wish I had received before bootstrapping my startup to $28M in ARR and $10M in EBITDA

1- Stop comparing yourself to others
I’ve met people who skyrocketed to $1M in ARR in 1 month and then plateaued… 
While others struggled for 7 years to reach their first $1M in ARR and then skyrocketed to $10M in less than 2 years.
Each journey is different.

2- Everything compounds
Little actions can compound BIG times over time. 
That works with sales and marketing but also with toxic behaviors. 
So make sure to watch for both, as they can either kill your company or make it skyrocket 🚀

3- Growth is a S-curve. If you don’t plan for it - you’ll plateau
Getting initial traction is extremely hard. 
But I thought that once we had reached product market fit, it would be a walk in the park… In reality, it’s not.
You always need to find new ways to find customers. Otherwise, you’ll plateau.

4- Love the game
Don’t hate competitors. Business is not a “war”.
You win a lot more when you enjoy the ride and meet cool people along the way.
Business is a game, and it should be fun. 
If it’s not, change the rules.

5- What took you here won’t take you there…
Doing more of the same thing is great, BUT only for a certain time. If you don’t try and experiment ruthlessly, you’ll get stuck. Innovation is the key to continuous fast growth.

6- Don’t compete, be unique
Peter Thiel said, “Competition is for losers”. And the best way to have 0 competition is to be unique. To be unique, be yourself, be passionate about the problem you solve, and help as many people as you can.

7- Document everything from day 1
Everyone believes that Marco Polo discovered the Silk Road. 
He didn’t. 
BUT, he was the first to document it. 
The more you document your journey, the more people will connect with you and trust you.

8- If it doesn’t kill you, don’t overreact
I used to see every single problem or bump in the road as the end of the world. In the end, if you switch your mindset to a solution-centric approach, you’ll see that there isn’t any problem in the world that can’t be solved.

9- Education is the best marketing
I’ve realized that building a SaaS to solve a problem is not enough. 
Education is far more important and can be a huge driver for growth (acquisition + retention). 
If you show people how to solve their problem and it works - then paying for your product or service will be a no-brainer.
